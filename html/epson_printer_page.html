Printer cartridges and toners are classified according to their yield. A page yield corresponds to the number of pages a single cartridge can print with.


Standard Yield is the most common variation of printer inks, containing a volume capable of 120 to 300 pages. This print volume standard inks cover a serviceable amount, but needs to be replaced often.


High yield, on the other hand, has more printing volume capability than the standard yield. Typically, it can print additional 50% or more pages versus the standard yield. The cartridges for both are usually same in size, but the latter contains more ink. They are also often labeled with XL.

OEM
OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer) inks are printer inks that are originally from Epson and are considered branded products. As such, these are more expensive than the non-OEM counterparts. While OEM manufacturers promise the best quality in the market than aftermarket ink cartridges, this is not exactly the case. The manufacturing method for OEM printer cartridge replacements and aftermarket cartridges are the same, which means they have the same quality.


What makes the original Epson ink cartridge more expensive than aftermarket cartridges?
As you might have noticed, printers are often sold at a cheaper price than how much they are supposed to be priced. Printer manufacturers would then take the profit from selling the ink cartridges at a much larger price. This somewhat makes it unfair as you are paying more than what you get. Here’s where aftermarket cartridges like remanufactured inks and compatible inks come to the rescue.


Remanufactured Ink Cartridges
Remanufactured printer cartridges are manufactured by third-party manufacturers which sell their products at a cheaper cost. These are cartridges that have only undergone one-cycle use, recycled and professionally reprocessed in a certified facility. As such, they are made from branded shells which are refilled with quality inks.
Since remanufactured inks are recycled, you are not only buying inks at a cheaper cost but are also helping the environment reduce landfill wastes.

Compatible Ink Cartridges
These are new ink cartridges manufactured by professionals and are quality tested to provide the same quality as an OEM cartridge. Despite the differences in the manufacturing method, the cartridges are built to seamlessly fit in your printer. They are also designed to work like the OEM cartridge, providing the same number of prints.

Troubleshooting Tips

Here are some troubleshooting tips for Epson printers and ink cartridges.

Third-party Epson ink cartridges such as compatible and remanufactured cartridges can be a bit tricky to install, and there are installation steps to take to benefit from these cost-effective ink cartridges. In many instances, the printer may reject a cloned cartridge, and some things can be done to reconfigure the device’s settings and allow the use of these units.

  1. Begin by installing the third-party ink cartridge, and the printer will display an “ink cartridge cannot be recognized” message.
  2. Go to “Start,” then proceed to “Control Panel” and click on “printer.”
  3. Search the “Epson Printer” icon, right-click on it and choose “Printer Preferences,” generally located at the bottom of the dialog box.
  4. Click the tab that shows “Speed and Progress” and locate your printer’s monitor box.
  5. Check off “Disable Epson Status Monitor” to turn the ink monitor off.
  6. Click “Save” to close the window.

That’s it when installing a cloned ink cartridge to your printer – you can now begin printing immediately.

Your Epson printer may say “ink cartridge not recognized” because inkjet cartridges come with electronic chips that help monitor the printing procedure and automatically shut down if the operation doesn’t follow the regular procedure.


Reconfiguring your printer settings is the only way to proceed under such circumstances. The Epson Printer can be reconfigured to print using cloned inkjet cartridges.

Find your Epson printer’s control panel. It’s usually located at your printer’s top right-hand side, close to any digital display screen your printer may have, and the usual location of the button used to turn your computer ON and OFF.
